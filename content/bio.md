Pauline Pelagia Berezina (b. 1994, Pskov, Russia), previously known as Pauline Garbbha, works on the edge of artistic research, site-specific art, phenomenology and griefwork, from the body as a site of political resonance.

Her artistic method is grounded in direct contact. She works in sites – whether a place or a person – marked by ecological, social, political or emotional catastrophe. She carries her Real of it to the viewer by means of photography and performance. Uneven surface, collapsed structures, cold water, high wind, fences: the body encounters them before the camera does. 

Berezina’s practice evolved from moments when the body and thought collided. During her first Vipassana course, dissociation suddenly fell away and the breath arrived as the proof of her existence, only at twenty-one instead of in childhood. Other discoveries came through injuries: broken arms, a car accident, abortion, sport traumas, self-destructive behaviours driven by untreated and undiagnosed mental disorders (BPD, GAD). The body’s limits and collapses became a method. Later, working with clay, she relied on mistakes – explosions in the kiln, ruptures to be not covered but reimagined, materials decomposing.

>She left Russia in 2020, closing her studio in Saint Petersburg and crossing the border under the weight of protest suppression, police violence and a little backpack (half of it taken by the camera), having the bodily certainty that something worse was coming. Exile was not a romantic rupture but a material one: 600 dollars, no work and no one waiting for her in Georgia; then living remotely from other migrants, only surrounded by a few locals, forest and silence. Displacement – violent, improvised, undignified – did not begin in 2020; it simply clarified the conditions she had lived in for years.

>Berezina grew up in a working-class family set by both the discipline and volatility of the 1990s in Russia. She recalls knowing her mother’s mood from the sound of the key turning in the door, dreaming late nights how to exit the domestic reality – through winning competitions or the window. The drive to appear “normal,” the violence used to enforce it, and the kid’s high sensitivity: this early mismatch produced both endurance and alienation, the twin forces that continue to shape her artistic language. 

>She first trained as a researcher in Existential Psychology at St. Petersburg State University (2012-2015, peak [experience](https://cestlaberezina.github.io/-/experience) and [death](https://cestlaberezina.github.io/-/death) narrative research, [in-depth](https://cestlaberezina.github.io/-/in-depth) interview method), working simultaneously at the crisis helpline and for female reproductive health research, practicing experimental theatre, contact improvisation, living and working in an intentional community, co-organizing experimental philosophical schools and collaborating with eco-villages. Her university research – phenomenological, metaphor-driven, not in need of positivist measurement – was dismissed. The rejection exposed what she already sensed: the institution was a patriarchal hierarchy that treated intuition and dissent as threats, with no curiosity or dialogue within its walls. Leaving triggered a break that reorganized her life: art became a survival structure rather than a pursuit.

She also developed practices of end-of-life & grief support (certified in 2023) and an intense six-year Lacanian analysis as a patient, which informs the way she approaches humans: not as models, but as subjects owning their lives and deaths, and as parts of nature.

Her concerns are rooted in structural trauma: state violence; land exhausted by capitalism; the everyday misogyny, stigma and social punishment directed at women; the horror of muteness and invisibility being foreign to any society, no matter the country; borders that cross humans. For Berezina, mental disorders are not private problems but political facts – conditions produced and intensified by environments that criminalize sensitivity and difference, stripping subjects down to function.

>"i believe that war happens first and perpetually inside, fever is the body’s way to pray and any word is a gesture. for me matters: questioning as an action, collapse at the end of the step, simplicity of tears, what turns grief into beauty, when people move their feet to let me pass to my seat in the theater, wander, reverie, heart, how to hold open the door in which i stand, intercellular space, how to go on, worship, how the space is filled with some kind of electricity, how to walk in one’s own unbought skin, how to resist the loss of wonder, what to do when no one is to blame, nausea, removal of distance, when something does not begin, how to fly underwater, abodes."

Berezina’s practice spans Russia, Georgia, Turkey, Spain and France. It rests on three axes: photographic translation of lived experience; somatic and grief work; phenomenological research. Across all of it runs the same conviction: the body, especially one that is vulnerable or foreign in any sense, is a way of understanding contemporary life—its violence, its tenderness, and its contradictions.
