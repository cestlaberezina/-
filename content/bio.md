Pauline Pelagia Berezina (b. 1994, Pskov, Russia), previously known as Pauline Garbbha, works on the edge of artistic research, [[site-specific]] art, [[phenomen]]ology and [[griefwork]], from the body as a site of [[politic]]al [[resonan]]ce.

Her artistic method is grounded in direct [[contact]]. She works in sites – whether a place or a person – marked by ecological, social, political or emotional [[catastroph]]e. She carries her [[Real]] of it to the viewer by means of photography and performance. Uneven surface, [[collaps]]ed structures, cold [[water]], high wind, fences: the body encounters them before the camera does. 

Berezina’s practice evolved from moments when the [[body]] and thought collided. During her first Vipassana course, [[dissociation]] suddenly fell away and the [[breath]] arrived as the proof of her [[exist]]ence, only at twenty-one instead of in childhood. Other discoveries came through injuries: broken arms, a car accident, abortion, sport traumas, self-destructive behaviours driven by untreated and undiagnosed mental disorders (BPD, GAD). The body’s [[limit]]s and collapses became a [[method]]. Later, working with clay, she relied on [[mistak]]es – explosions in the kiln, [[rupture]]s to be not covered but reimagined, materials decomposing.

>She left Russia in 2020, closing her studio in Saint Petersburg and crossing the border under the weight of [[protest]] [[suppress]]ion, police [[violence]] and a little backpack (half of it taken by the camera), having the bodily certainty that something worse was coming. [[Exile]] was not a romantic rupture but a material one: 600 dollars, no work and no one waiting for her in Georgia; then living remotely from other migrants, only surrounded by a few locals, forest and [[silen]]ce. [[Displac]]ement – violent, improvised, undignified – did not begin in 2020; it simply clarified the conditions she had lived in for years.

>Berezina grew up in a working-class family set by both the [[disciplin]]e and volatility of the 1990s in Russia. She recalls knowing her [[mother]]’s [[mood]] from the [[sound]] of the key turning in the door, dreaming late nights how to exit the domestic [[reality]] – through winning competitions or the window. The [[drive]] to appear “normal,” the violence used to enforce it, and the kid’s high sensitivity: this early mismatch produced both endurance and [[alien]]ation, the twin forces that continue to shape her artistic language. 

>She first trained as a researcher in Existential Psychology at St. Petersburg State University (2012-2015, peak [experience](https://cestlaberezina.github.io/-/experience) and [death](https://cestlaberezina.github.io/-/death) narrative research, [in-depth](https://cestlaberezina.github.io/-/in-depth) interview method), working simultaneously at the [[cris]]is helpline and for [[fem]]ale reproductive health research, practicing [[experiment]]al [[theatre]], [[contact]] improvisation, living and working in an intentional [[commu]]nity, co-organizing experimental philosophical schools and collaborating with eco-villages. Her university research – [[phenomen]]ological, [[metaphor]]-driven, not in need of positivist measurement – was dismissed. The [[reject]]ion exposed what she already sensed: the institution was a [[patriarch]]al [[hierarchy]] that treated [[intuit]]ion and dissent as threats, with no [[curios]]ity or [[dialogue]] within its walls. Leaving triggered a break that reorganized her life: art became a [[surviv]]al structure rather than a pursuit.

She also developed practices of end-of-life & grief support (certified in 2023) and an intense six-year [[Lacan]]ian analysis as a patient, which informs the way she approaches [[human]]s: not as models, but as [[subject]]s owning their lives and [[death]]s, as parts of [[natur]]e.

Her concerns are rooted in structural [[trauma]]: state violence; land [[exhaust]]ed by [[capital]]ism; the everyday misogyny, [[stigma]] and [[social]] punishment directed at women; the [[horror]] of muteness and invisibility being [[foreign]] to any society, no matter the country; [[border]]s that cross humans. For Berezina, mental [[disorder]]s are not private problems but political facts – conditions produced and intensified by [[environment]]s that criminalize sensitivity and difference, stripping subjects down to [[function]].

>"i believe that [[war]] happens first and perpetually inside and [[fever]] is the [[body]]’s way to [[pray]]. for me matters: [[question]]ing as an [[act]]ion, [[collaps]]e at the [[end]] of the [[step]], simplicity of [[tear]]s, what [[turn]]s [[grief]] into [[beauty]], when people [[move]] their feet to let me [[pass]] to my seat in the [[theatre]], [[wander]], [[reverie]], [[heart]], how to [[hold]] [[open]] the [[door]] in which i stand, [[intercellular]] [[space]], how to go on, [[worship]], how the space is [[fill]]ed with some kind of [[electricity]], how to [[walk]] in my own unbought [[skin]], how to [[resist]] the [[loss]] of [[wonder]], what to do when no one is to [[blame]], [[nausea]], removal of [[distan]]ce, when something does not [[begin]], how to [[fly]] [[underwater]], [[abode]]s."

Berezina’s practice spans Russia, Georgia, Turkey, Spain and France. It rests on three axes: photographic translation of lived experience; somatic and grief work; phenomenological research. Across all of it runs the same conviction: the body, especially one that is vulnerable or foreign in any sense, is a way of understanding contemporary life – its violence, its [[tender]]ness, and its contradictions.
